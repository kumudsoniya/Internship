<h2 class="mp-h2" id="mp-tfa-h2"><span id="From_today.27s_featured_article"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="From_today's_featured_article">From today's featured article</span></h2>
<div class="mp-contains-float" id="mp-tfa"><div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 140px;">
<span typeof="mw:File"><a class="mw-file-description" href="/wiki/File:Smooth_Toadfish-Tetractenos_glaber_(mirrored).JPG" title="Smooth toadfish"><img alt="Smooth toadfish" class="mw-file-element" data-file-height="1072" data-file-width="1072" decoding="async" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Smooth_Toadfish-Tetractenos_glaber_%28mirrored%29.JPG/140px-Smooth_Toadfish-Tetractenos_glaber_%28mirrored%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Smooth_Toadfish-Tetractenos_glaber_%28mirrored%29.JPG/210px-Smooth_Toadfish-Tetractenos_glaber_%28mirrored%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Smooth_Toadfish-Tetractenos_glaber_%28mirrored%29.JPG/280px-Smooth_Toadfish-Tetractenos_glaber_%28mirrored%29.JPG 2x" width="140"/></a></span></div>

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Smooth_toadfish" title="Smooth toadfish">smooth toadfish</a></b> (<i>Tetractenos glaber</i>) is a species in the pufferfish family <a href="/wiki/Tetraodontidae" title="Tetraodontidae">Tetraodontidae</a>. It is native to shallow coastal and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuarine</a> waters of southeastern Australia, where it is widespread and abundant. French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Christophe-Paulin_de_La_Poix_de_Fr%C3%A9minville" title="Christophe-Paulin de La Poix de Frminville">Christophe-Paulin de La Poix de Frminville</a> described the species in 1813, though early records confused it with what is now the only other member of its <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Common_toadfish" title="Common toadfish">common toadfish</a> (<i>T. hamiltoni</i><span style="padding-left: 0.1em">)</span>. Up to <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1154941027">.mw-parser-output .frac{white-space:nowrap}.mw-parser-output .frac .num,.mw-parser-output .frac .den{font-size:80%;line-height:0;vertical-align:super}.mw-parser-output .frac .den{vertical-align:sub}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);clip-path:polygon(0px 0px,0px 0px,0px 0px);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style>16 cm (<span class="frac" role="math">6<span class="sr-only">+</span><span class="num">1</span><span class="den">4</span></span> in) long with distinctive leopard-like dark markings on its upperparts, the smooth toadfish has a flattened belly and an elongate body tapering to a slender tail. Its back and fins are rounded. Unlike most of its relatives, it does not have prominent spines on its body. Like other pufferfish, it can inflate itself with water or air. It forages for its preferred foods<a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>in sandy or muddy <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a>. The smooth toadfish's flesh contains the poison <a href="/wiki/Tetrodotoxin" title="Tetrodotoxin">tetrodotoxin</a>, and eating it can be fatal. (<b><a href="/wiki/Smooth_toadfish" title="Smooth toadfish">Full article...</a></b>)

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Red-tailed_squirrel" title="Red-tailed squirrel">red-tailed squirrel</a></b> (<i>Sciurus granatensis</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Tree_squirrel" title="Tree squirrel">tree squirrel</a>. It is found in Central and South America  including Colombia, Costa Rica, Ecuador, Panama, and Venezuela  and the Caribbean islands of Trinidad and Tobago and <a href="/wiki/Margarita_Island" title="Margarita Island">Margarita</a>. It has also been introduced and is invasive in Cuba, but this is a small population found around some parts of the margin of Rio Almendares in Havana that escaped from the Havana Zoo. This is a common squirrel with a stable population and a vast range consisting of different habitats, including human-influenced environments, thus it is not thought to be under threat.
